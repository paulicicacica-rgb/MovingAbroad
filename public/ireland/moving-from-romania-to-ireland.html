Is it worth moving from Romania to Ireland?

The honest answer

Moving to Ireland from Romania makes financial sense if you earn in IT, healthcare, or skilled trades — you'll earn 40-60% more and build career experience that's valued globally. Quality of life is genuinely good: better public services, safer streets, no corruption stress. But here's the truth: Ireland is expensive (rent is 2-3x Romania's), the weather is genuinely depressing (rain 200 days yearly), and you'll feel isolated unless you actively build a community. This move suits ambitious 25-35 year-olds with strong English who want career growth and can handle 3-5 years of grey skies. It's less suitable if you're close to family, struggle with cold climates, or just want an easy life abroad — Portugal or Spain might suit you better.

Visa & permits for Romania citizens

  • No visa needed for short visits.** Romanian citizens have freedom of movement within the EU, so you don't need a visa to enter Ireland — just your valid Romanian passport or ID card.
  • Work visa/Employment Permit.** If you have a job offer, your employer can sponsor an Employment Permit. You'll need a salary of €32,000+ per year (€40,000+ for non-critical skills list), and processing takes 4-8 weeks. Your employer applies, not you — this is crucial.
  • Critical Skills Employment Permit.** If you're in tech, healthcare, or engineering earning €32,000+, this is faster (2-3 weeks) and easier. Medical doctors, nurses, and software engineers are on Ireland's critical skills list.
  • Self-employment/Sponsorship.** If starting a business, you apply directly to the Department of Enterprise, Trade and Employment. You need a business plan, proof of funds (€10,000+), and it takes 4-6 weeks. Costs €200.
  • Student visa route.** Study a full-time course (€6,000-15,000 yearly), get a student visa valid for course duration plus 2 months. Post-graduation, you get an automatic 2-year work permit.
  • Family/Partner sponsorship.** If your Irish/EU partner sponsors you, this is the fastest route (2-3 weeks). They need to prove cohabitation and income of €30,000+. Spouse visas are effectively automatic for EU citizens currently.

Before you leave Romania

Get your Romanian birth certificate, education diplomas, and professional qualifications officially translated and apostilled in Romania — you'll need these for Irish employers and authorities. Open a Romanian bank account or keep one active so you can send money back home later without fees or delays. If you own property or a car in Romania, decide now: sell it, rent it out, or arrange remote management, because dealing with property remotely is complicated. Notify the Romanian tax authority (ANAF) that you're leaving and update your residency status to avoid paying Romanian taxes on worldwide income. Research and book your first month's accommodation in Ireland NOW — prices spike 2-3 weeks before your travel date, and last-minute accommodation is scams or terrible. Get a comprehensive health insurance quote from Romania (international expat policies start €40/month) before leaving, as Irish health insurance won't cover pre-existing conditions during waiting periods. Create a budget spreadsheet with your actual Romanian salary converted to euros so you understand the real purchasing power change. Set up Google alerts for your target city and job sector so you start recognising salary ranges and company names before you arrive. Download offline maps of Irish cities and install the Leap card app (Dublin transport) before arrival.

First week in Ireland

  • Day 1:** Arrive at Dublin Airport (if flying). Buy a Leap card (€10 refundable, €5 credit) at the airport or on the bus — it's how you'll use taxis, buses, and trains for the next week. Get a 3 mobile SIM card at the airport (€20 for phone + €10 credit, €7.99/month for 40GB data) because you need mobile internet immediately for accommodation searches. Head to your pre-booked hostel or Airbnb using your Leap card on the Aircoach bus to the city centre (€8, 30 minutes). Spend the evening walking your neighbourhood, finding a supermarket, and charging your phone. Don't try to explore everything — you're jet-lagged and overwhelmed.
  • Day 2-3:** Start apartment hunting seriously using Daft.ie, SpareRoom.ie, and Facebook groups like "Apartment Dublin," "Dublin Accommodation," and your target city. Most apartments list on Wednesday-Friday, so check multiple times daily. Budget €700-900 for a single room in shared houses, €1,200+ for a studio. Video call landlords before visiting (many are reliable, but in-person viewings get priority). Visit 5-8 apartments over these two days. Bring a completed viewing form with references from previous landlords (email your Romanian landlord for a reference). Most landlords want a holding deposit (€300-500) and first month's rent upfront — be ready to pay immediately when you find something decent.
  • Day 4-5:** Move into your apartment or secure one. Register immediately — don't wait. Go to your local Garda Station (police station) with your passport and proof of address (rental contract) to register as a resident. This takes 10 minutes and gives you the Garda Number you'll need for everything else. Open a bank account at AIB, Bank of Ireland, or Revolut. Revolut is fastest (30 minutes, online), but traditional banks give you an Irish sort code needed for some employers. Visit a supermarket and buy the essentials: tea, milk, bread, basic kitchen stuff.
  • Day 6-7:** Find your local GP surgery by searching "GP near me" and register. You might not get an appointment for 2 weeks, but registering now means you're in the system. Walk your neighbourhood properly — find the good coffee shops, parks, and where Romanians gather (check Facebook groups). Join 2-3 local Facebook groups for your city and introduce yourself. Attend one community event if there's one happening. Buy a weekly bus/train pass if you haven't already. By day 7, you should have an apartment booked or secured, a phone number, a bank account opened, and a GP registration started.

First month essentials

  • 1. Personal Public Service Number (PPS).** Go to your local Intreo office (Department of Enterprise, Trade and Employment) with your passport, proof of address (rental contract), and a completed application form (you can download it or fill it at the office). Bring your Garda registration number. Processing takes 2-4 weeks. Cost: free. This number is your Irish tax/ID number and you'll need it for everything.
  • 2. Bank account.** Revolut, Wise, or a traditional bank (AIB/BOI). Revolut takes 30 minutes online and is perfect for international transfers. Traditional banks take 1 hour, need proof of address and your PPS number (you'll use Garda registration initially). You'll get a sort code (for employers) and IBAN. Most employers will transfer salary automatically once you give them bank details.
  • 3. Healthcare registration.** Go to your local GP surgery, fill out a registration form, and wait. Public healthcare (through your GP) is free once you register — no payment upfront. Private insurance costs €40-80/month but gives you faster specialist access and dental coverage. For emergencies, go to any hospital A&E (emergency room) — public healthcare covers this automatically. Get your GP appointment within 4 weeks because you'll likely need a travel vaccination update or health check.
  • 4. Driving licence transfer.** If you have a Romanian licence, go to your local driving test centre with your Romanian licence, passport, and proof of address. Irish transfer takes 2-3 weeks and costs €55. You'll get a photo ID Irish licence valid for 10 years. If your Romanian licence is old-style (not EU card format), you'll need to take a theory test first (€35). Start this immediately because many employers ask for a valid driving licence.
  • 5. Qualification recognition.** If you're in a regulated profession (healthcare, engineering, law, teaching), contact the relevant Irish regulatory body immediately. Nurses register with the Nursing and Midwifery Board of Ireland (NMBI), doctors with the Medical Council, engineers with Engineers Ireland. Processing takes 4-12 weeks and costs €100-500. Non-regulated qualifications (IT, marketing, business) don't need formal recognition, but get a notarised translation done (€20-40) for your CV.

Money & cost of living in Ireland

  • Monthly budget for a single person in Dublin (after-tax, realistic 2024):**
  • Tight budget (€1,950/month):** Rent €650 (shared house suburbs), groceries €200, utilities €100, transport €80 (monthly pass), phone €10, eating out €200, entertainment €60, health insurance €0 (public only), miscellaneous €650. This is genuine survival mode — you're not going out much, you're not dating, you're cycling to work.
  • Comfortable budget (€2,650/month):** Rent €900 (own studio/apartment, south Dublin), groceries €280, utilities €140, transport €100, phone €15, eating out €400, entertainment €150, health insurance €50, clothes/personal €100, savings €515. This is realistic for someone earning €35,000-40,000 gross. You're living decently, going out once weekly, saving €500/month.
  • Good lifestyle (€3,400/month):** Rent €1,200 (nice 1-bed city centre), groceries €350, utilities €170, transport €120, phone €20, eating out €700, entertainment €300, health insurance €80, clothes/personal €200, socialising €260. This requires €50,000+ gross salary. You're going out 2-3 times weekly, travelling monthly, building a real life.
  • Rent reality:** City centre Dublin rooms €750-950, shared houses €650-800, studios €1,100-1,500, 1-bed apartments €1,200-1,800. Outside Dublin (Cork, Galway, Limerick): 30-40% cheaper. Prices have increased 15-20% yearly for 3 years.
  • Sending money back to Romania.** Wise (formerly TransferWise) charges 1.5% + €0.5, takes 1-2 business days, gives real exchange rates — best for frequent transfers. Revolut charges 1-2%, instant. Bank transfers cost €10-15 and take 5-7 days. For €500 to Romania: Wise costs €8.50, bank costs €15. Use Wise.
  • Tax treaty:** Ireland-Romania tax treaty means no double taxation on salary income. You'll pay Irish tax only (20-40% depending on income). Romania won't tax you if you're non-resident (which you are after moving).

Finding work in Ireland

  • Top 5 hiring sectors for Romanians in Ireland:**
  • 1. IT/Software development.** 6,000+ jobs available. Average salary: €45,000-65,000 (Dublin) for mid-level, €65,000-95,000 for senior. Companies like Google, Meta, Microsoft, Apple, Amazon all have Irish offices. Your Romanian CV is valued — Irish companies know Romania produces excellent engineers. Entry requirement: 2+ years experience or CS degree.
  • 2. Healthcare (nursing & medicine).** 3,000+ nurse positions unfilled. Salary: €32,000-38,000 starting, €45,000+ after 5 years. Doctors: €45,000-65,000 depending on specialisation. Huge demand. Requires NMBI registration (4-12 weeks). Your Romanian qualifications are recognised if you've practised for 2+ years.
  • 3. Engineering (construction, civil, mechanical).** 2,500+ positions. Salary: €40,000-55,000 starting, €60,000-80,000 senior. Registration with Engineers Ireland takes 4 weeks (€200-300). Romanian engineers are highly sought because Ireland has construction booms ongoing.
  • 4. Sales & business development.** 2,000+ roles. Salary: €30,000-40,000 + commission (commission can double your salary). Less formal qualification recognition needed. Companies value multilingual salespeople — your Romanian + English is an asset.
  • 5. Manufacturing/skilled trades.** 1,500+ roles. Salary: €28,000-38,000. Welders, electricians, plumbers needed urgently. Less competition from other expats. Your trade qualifications are recognised if you have practical experience.
  • Job search sites:** LinkedIn (best for quality roles), Indeed.ie, Jobs.ie, IrisJobs.ie. Set alerts for your sector and location. Most jobs posted Wednesday-Friday.
  • Interview culture differences.** Irish interviews are less formal than you expect — they'll ask about your personality and cultural fit, not just technical knowledge. They value humility. Don't oversell yourself; honesty goes further. Salary negotiation is expected — don't accept the first offer.
  • After-tax salary reality.** €35,000 gross = €27,500 net (21% tax + 8.5% social insurance). €50,000 gross = €37,500 net. €65,000 gross = €46,500 net. This is significantly more than Romania (where same roles pay €15,000-25,000), but Ireland's cost of living eats 50-60% of your net salary on basics.
  • Romania experience is valued.** Irish employers know Romania has quality professionals. Your experience isn't discounted — it's actually a plus because it shows you survived a harder business environment.

Life in Ireland — what to expect

  • Weather reality.** Ireland is grey, wet, and cold October-March. Expect rain 200+ days yearly. Winter (December-February) has 7-8 hours of daylight. Summer is 17-18 hours. Many Romanians struggle with this — vitamin D deficiency and seasonal depression are real. Budget for a SAD light (€30) and proper rain gear (€80-120). It's not dramatic, it's just relentlessly grey.
  • Housing market tips and scams.** Daft.ie and SpareRoom.ie are legitimate. Avoid WhatsApp-only landlords, anyone asking for deposits via Western Union, or prices 20% below market rate — these are scams. Legitimate landlords use contracts, registered with Residential Tenancies Board (RTB), and ask for normal deposits (€300-500). Don't sign anything without seeing the property in person. Rental deposits are protected by law — demand a receipt. Scammers are common; trust your gut.
  • Where Romanians live.** Dublin south side (Ranelagh, Rathmines, Ballsbridge) is expensive but has better amenities. Dublin west (Ballymun, Blanchardstown) is cheaper but further. Cork city centre has a growing Romanian community. Galway has fewer Romanians but a younger international vibe. Most cluster in Dublin because jobs are there.
  • Community and Facebook groups.** Join "Romanians in Ireland" (3,000+ members), "Dublin Accommodation," and city-specific groups. These are genuinely helpful — people trade jobs, apartments, advice. Attend meetups if you're lonely. Real friendships form here.
  • Cultural surprises.** Irish people are friendly but not immediately intimate — they take weeks to warm up properly. They don't discuss religion or politics on first meetings. They love self-deprecating humour. Pubs are social centres, not just bars — you'll spend a lot there. Queuing is sacred. Customer service is slower than Romania — don't expect quick problem-solving. Bureaucracy moves slowly despite seeming simpler.
  • Finding Romanian food.** Carrefour, Dunnes Stores, and ethnic shops stock Eastern European basics. Romanian restaurants exist in Dublin (try "Transylvania" on Parliament Street or search Facebook). But expect 3x Romanian prices. Most Romanians cook at home — this saves money and helps homesickness.
  • Transport.** Dublin buses and trams (Luas) use Leap cards. Monthly pass: €130 (bus) or €104 (rail). Cycling is popular and cheap. Outside Dublin, you'll likely need a car — petrol costs €1.40/litre. Car insurance: €400-700/year for young drivers.
  • Healthcare quality.** Public GPs are good but slow (2-3 week waits for appointments). A&E (emergency) is free but wait times are 4-6 hours. Private healthcare is available but expensive (€200+ per GP visit). Prescriptions cost €11.50 per item regardless of price. Overall quality is better than Romania but the system feels slower.
  • Expat happiness.** Most Romanians who stay 3+ years love Ireland. Those who leave do so because of weather, isolation, or homesickness. Building a social life takes 6-12 months of intentional effort — it doesn't happen automatically. Financially, almost everyone says it was worth it. The key is staying 3-5 years minimum to make the stress worthwhile.
