Is it worth moving from Italy to Ireland?

The honest answer

Moving from Italy to Ireland makes sense if you want higher wages, career growth in tech or healthcare, and English-language opportunities—but not if you value Mediterranean climate, affordable housing, or close family nearby. You'll earn 30-40% more in Dublin than most Italian cities, but rent costs 50% more and you'll miss the food culture badly. Quality of life is good: safe cities, efficient services, friendly people—yet Ireland feels less "lived-in" than Italy, with colder weather nine months yearly and a more reserved social style. This move suits ambitious 25-40 year-olds seeking career advancement, remote workers with international salaries, and people prioritizing financial security over lifestyle. The real con: you're trading sunshine, wine culture, and family proximity for professional opportunity and better long-term savings potential.

Visa & permits for Italy citizens

  • EU Citizens Post-Brexit:** You need a visa. As an Italian citizen, you cannot live or work in Ireland indefinitely without permission anymore. Processing typically takes 4-8 weeks.
  • Critical Employment Permit (most common):** For jobs paying €32,000+ annually (€64,000+ for non-listed occupations). You need a job offer first, your employer applies, and it costs €1,000. Processing: 4 weeks typically. Valid two years, renewable. Healthcare and family reunification included.
  • Start-Up Employment Permit:** If you're starting a business with €50,000 investment, you can get a two-year visa. Requires business plan approval from Enterprise Ireland.
  • Intra-Company Transfer:** Moving within the same company to their Irish office. Faster (2 weeks), but employer must sponsor. No salary threshold.
  • Student Route:** Full-time study (€9,000-25,000 yearly tuition) grants a two-year visa post-graduation for work search. Part-time work allowed (20 hours weekly during term).
  • Spouse/Partner Route:** If you have an Irish citizen or settled partner, you can apply for Dependent/Partner status. Requires proof of relationship (minimum one year), combined income €25,000+, and housing proof. Takes 8-12 weeks.
  • Digital Nomad Visa:** Doesn't exist in Ireland currently, unlike Portugal or Croatia.
  • Bilateral Agreements:** Italy-Ireland have no special fast-track agreement since Brexit. Standard EU rules don't apply.

Before you leave Italy

Get an Italian residence certificate (certificato di residenza) from your comune—you'll need this for Irish visa applications and employer verification. Contact your Italian tax authority (Agenzia delle Entrate) to notify them of your departure; you may need a final tax return if self-employed. Close or downgrade your Italian phone contract (early termination fees exist; check your provider's specific terms). Transfer or sell your car if you own one, or arrange shipment if you're bringing it (expensive: €1,500-3,000 via ferry). Notify your Italian bank and request international transfer access; set up online banking NOW before leaving. Gather original copies of diplomas, work certificates, and professional qualifications—get them officially translated into English (€50-150 per document) as you'll need them for job applications. Research and tentatively book temporary housing for your first 2-4 weeks (Airbnb, hostels, or Facebook groups for short lets—expect €40-60 nightly). Open a small Irish bank account online before arriving (some banks like Revolut or Wise work from Italy) to avoid the first-week cash problem. Create a detailed budget spreadsheet: calculate your UK pounds/euros equivalent so salary offers in euros make sense, not shock you. Book your flight for a Tuesday-Thursday arrival if possible—you'll have time that week to start admin without weekend delays.

First week in Ireland

  • Day 1 (Tuesday arrival):** Head directly from the airport to a convenience store or Eir shop and buy an Irish SIM card (€20, includes €5 credit; Eir or Three are reliable). This is urgent—you need a local number. Purchase a Leap Card at Dublin Airport station (€5, reloadable) for bus/tram/train access across the city. Take a taxi or bus to your temporary accommodation; don't rely on your Italian phone's GPS yet. Check into your Airbnb or hostel, confirm WiFi access, and locate the nearest supermarket (Tesco, Dunnes, or Lidl). Eat somewhere simple tonight—jet lag is real, and you need rest more than exploration.
  • Day 2-3 (Wednesday-Thursday):** Spend these days viewing apartments seriously. Use Daft.ie, SpareRoom.ie, and Facebook groups ("Dublin Apartments," "Dublin Housing"). Target areas: Stoneybatter, Rathmines, or Ballymun if south side; Clontarf or Drumcondra if north. Rent runs €800-1,200 for a one-bed city centre, €600-900 in suburbs. Book three viewings per day, take photos of everything, and ask landlords about deposit protection (legally required—ask for the TenancyRegistration number). Never transfer money before visiting in person; deposit scams are real. Most leases require first month's rent plus one month's deposit upfront (€1,400-2,400 total for most places). Arrange viewings via WhatsApp—Irish landlords expect this.
  • Day 4-5 (Friday-Saturday):** If you've found an apartment, sign the lease and transfer deposit Friday. Saturday, buy basics: bedding (Dunnes or IKEA), kitchen essentials, toiletries. Irish landlords almost never provide these. Visit your local Garda (police) station to register your address—immigration requires this within three months, and doing it early avoids last-minute stress. Bring your passport, visa approval letter, and rental agreement. Registration is free and takes 20 minutes. This weekend, also open an Irish bank account in person (AIB, Bank of Ireland, or Ulster Bank all accept new residents; bring passport, Irish address proof—rental agreement works—and €10 minimum deposit). Processing takes 5-10 minutes for basic accounts.
  • Day 6-7 (Sunday-Monday, second week):** Register with a GP (general practitioner) for public healthcare access. Use your local health service office or search the Irish Health Service Executive (HSE) website. You'll need your Irish address, passport, and proof of eligibility (your visa or employment letter). Registration is free but can take 2-3 weeks for full access; some GPs have waiting lists. Don't wait until you're sick. Monday, explore your neighbourhood on foot: locate the nearest Tesco or Dunnes for groceries, find your local pub (Irish social life centres here), walk to work if you've started, and identify the nearest bus/tram stop. By day 7, you should feel geographically oriented, have functioning utilities, and know where to buy essentials.

First month essentials

  • 1. Irish Tax Number (PPS Number):** This is your Personal Public Service Number—Ireland's tax ID and essential for everything. Visit your nearest Department of Social Protection office (there's one in Dublin's Gardiner Street, similar offices nationwide). Bring your passport, Irish address proof (rental agreement), and a completed SR1 form (download from welfare.ie). Processing is immediate if you're there in person; you'll leave with a temporary number and receive the official card in 5-7 business days by post. It's free. You cannot work officially without this, so prioritize it—do this in your first week if possible, not later.
  • 2. Bank Account:** You should have opened a basic current account on arrival (Day 5), but now set up a full account if needed. Best banks for newcomers: AIB and Bank of Ireland offer straightforward online onboarding; Wise (if you need international transfers back to Italy) or Revolut (already has your data from before arriving). Most require €10-50 minimum deposit. You'll need your PPS number, passport, Irish address, and proof of employment or student status. Online accounts open in 10 minutes; physical branches take 15 minutes. Irish banks rarely charge monthly fees for standard accounts. Set up a standing order to your Italian bank account immediately—it'll process faster if your Italian account is already "receiving" transfers from Ireland. International transfers cost €5-10 per transaction with most banks; Wise charges 0.7% (cheaper for large amounts).
  • 3. Healthcare (HSE/Public):** If you registered with a GP on Day 6, you're in the public system. It's free at point of use once you're registered (no NHS-style insurance needed). Book a first appointment with your registered GP—it typically takes 2-3 weeks, so expect your first appointment around week 4. For emergencies, go directly to a hospital accident & emergency (A&E) department; this is free, but waits can be 4-8 hours for non-critical cases. Prescription costs are capped at €160 per item monthly (Medical Card holders pay €1.50; check if you qualify—most new workers don't immediately). Consider private health insurance (Laya, VHI, Irish Life) at €40-80 monthly for faster GP access and dental coverage; public doesn't cover dentistry or optometry. Private GP visits cost €50-80 without insurance.
  • 4. Driving Licence Transfer:** If you have an Italian driving licence and plan to drive, convert it to Irish. Contact the National Driver Licence Service (NDLS) in Donegal; apply online (ndls.ie) or by post. You'll need your Italian licence, passport, Irish address proof, and a completed D102 form. Cost is €55; processing takes 2-4 weeks. Your Italian licence works temporarily (up to 12 months), but Irish insurance won't quote until you have an Irish licence. Car insurance runs €600-1,200 yearly (higher in Dublin); get quotes from Allianz, AXA, or AAMI. If you don't drive, skip this entirely—Dublin's public transport is solid.
  • 5. Italy-Specific Qualifications:** If you're a doctor, lawyer, engineer, accountant, or other regulated professional, your Italian qualifications need recognition. Contact the relevant Irish regulatory body: Medical Council (doctors), Law Society (solicitors), Engineers Ireland, or Accountancy bodies. EU qualifications are generally recognized under mutual recognition directives, but you may need to sit an Irish exam in English, especially in law. This takes 2-12 weeks depending on profession. Non-regulated work (marketing, IT, sales) doesn't require this—your CV and references are enough. Start this process immediately if applicable; it's the slowest admin item and could delay your job start.

Money & cost of living in Ireland

  • Monthly Budget (Single Person, Dublin City Centre):**
  • Rent:** €950-1,200 (one-bed city centre, south side). Suburbs: €650-850. You'll also pay landlord insurance (included in rent or €50-100 yearly) and a one-time agency fee if using a lettings agent (€200-400, but direct landlords avoid this).
  • Groceries:** €250-300 monthly (Lidl or Dunnes for budget shopping; Italian food is 2-3x pricier—expect €6 for decent pasta, €8 for mozzarella). Coffee out: €3.50 per cappuccino.
  • Utilities (gas, electricity, water):** €100-150 monthly, higher October-March. Internet: €35-50 monthly.
  • Transport:** €100-140 monthly (unlimited bus/tram/train with a monthly Leap Card). Taxis: €8-15 across Dublin. No car needed in the city proper.
  • Phone (mobile only):** €20-40 monthly for unlimited data and calls.
  • Eating Out:** €15-25 per meal at casual restaurants; €60-100 for nice dinner for two. Pint of beer: €5.50-6.50.
  • Entertainment & Subscriptions:** €30-50 (cinema €12, gym €50 monthly, Netflix €13.99).
  • Healthcare:** €0 GP visits once registered (public), or €50-80 private. Private health insurance: €50-80 monthly (optional). Dental/optometry: €50-150 per visit (public doesn't cover).
  • Total, tight budget:** €1,800-2,000 monthly.
  • Total, comfortable:** €2,300-2,600 monthly.
  • Total, good lifestyle:** €3,000+ monthly.
  • Salary Expectations After Tax (Dublin 2024):**
  • Entry-level (marketing, sales, customer service): €28,000-32,000 gross = €22,500-25,000 net.
  • Mid-level (3-5 years experience): €40,000-50,000 gross = €31,000-38,000 net.
  • Senior (8+ years): €60,000-80,000 gross = €43,000-56,000 net.
  • Tax: 20% on income €0-23,000, then 40% above. You also pay 4% PRSI (social insurance) and 0.5% Universal Social Charge, meaning total deductions are 24.5%-44.5% depending on salary.
  • Sending Money to Italy:**
  • Best services: Wise (0.7% fee, excellent exchange rates), Revolut (0.5% fee for premium), or your Irish bank's international transfer (€5-10 flat fee, slower, worse rates). For €500-1,000 monthly to family, Wise saves €15-30 compared to your bank. Set up a standing order so it's automatic—this avoids the temptation to spend it. Italy-Ireland don't have a tax treaty affecting salary tax, but you may still owe Italian tax if you maintain property or assets there; check with a tax advisor (€100-150 consultation).
  • First Month Costs (Move-In Surge):**
  • Expect €2,500-3,500 upfront: rent + deposit (€1,400-2,400), initial furniture/bedding (€300-500), SIM card and essential groceries (€100), transport card (€5), GP registration and misc admin (€100). Budget conservatively; your first month is not representative of ongoing costs.

Finding work in Ireland

  • Top 5 Hiring Sectors:**
  • 1. **Tech/Software:** Dublin is a major tech hub (Google, Meta, Stripe, Airbnb offices here). Demand is fierce for developers, product managers, UX designers. Average salary: €55,000-90,000. Language: English only. Your Italian background means nothing; your portfolio/GitHub matters entirely. Job sites: LinkedIn, Stack Overflow Jobs, Dice.ie.
  • 2. **Healthcare:** Nurses, physiotherapists, radiographers severely needed. Average salary: €35,000-50,000. Qualification recognition required (1-12 weeks via Medical Council). Strong Irish demand; your Italian qualifications are respected.
  • 3. **Fintech/Finance:** Dublin hosts major EU banking (Deutsche Bank, AIB, Bank of Ireland, Transferwise). Accountants, analysts, traders wanted. Average salary: €50,000-85,000. Qualifications like CPA or ACCA recognized. Some roles require Irish tax/regulatory knowledge—your Italian background is neutral here.
  • 4. **Hospitality/Food Service:** Always hiring (restaurants, hotels, pubs). Average salary: €25,000-35,000 (lower than Italy surprisingly, but tips help). Your Italian accent helps in restaurants; many restaurants seek Italian chefs and servers. English essential.
  • 5. **Pharma/Life Sciences:** Cork and Dublin have major pharma plants. Researchers, manufacturing roles, quality assurance. Average salary: €40,000-65,000. Degrees in chemistry, biology, medicine recognized. Steady, less flashy than tech, but reliable.
  • Salary After Tax Reality:** A €50,000 gross offer = €38,000-39,000 net. A €35,000 offer = €28,000 net. Calculate this immediately when comparing to Italian offers.
  • Qualification Recognition:**
  • **Regulated professions (doctors, lawyers, engineers):** Must register with Irish body; expect 4-12 weeks and potential exam fees (€500-2,000). Start this before arriving.
  • **Unregulated (marketing, sales, IT, finance non-regulated roles):** Your CV and work experience matter; degrees less critical. Italian university degrees are respected but not required to be formally recognized.
  • Language Requirements:** English is essential for all roles. Italian is useful for hospitality/tourism but not required. Your English ability will be assessed in interviews; if you're not fluent, expect initial rejections.
  • Best Job Sites:** LinkedIn (filter by Ireland, Dublin), Dice.ie (tech-heavy), Indeed.ie, IrisJobs.ie, Workable. Many Irish companies recruit heavily on LinkedIn; set your profile to "open to work" immediately upon arrival.
  • Interview Culture Differences:** Much friendlier and less formal than Italy. Expect casual dress codes, first-name basis, and interviewers asking about your personality/interests (not just qualifications). They'll ask "Why Ireland?" and "Why us?"—have genuine answers. Salary negotiation is expected; offer €40,000? Counter with €42,000-43,000. They assume you will.
  • Italy Experience Valued?** Depends on role. Tech: almost irrelevant unless you worked at a major company. Finance/banking: respected if you worked at major Italian bank. Healthcare: very respected—Italian healthcare standards are high, and Irish employers know this. Hospitality: highly valued. Most other sectors: neutral. Don't oversell it; frame it as "I bring European perspective" rather than "Italy is better."
  • Realistic Timeline:** Job search takes 2-6 weeks from arrival if you start before leaving Italy (online applications). Interviews happen 1-2 weeks after application. Offers come 2 weeks later. Start applying 6-8 weeks before you want to move; arrive with a job lined up, not after.
  • Visa Note:** You need a job offer at €32,000+ to get a Critical Employment Permit. This is harsh—many entry-level roles fall below this. If you're moving on savings, you have some flexibility to take lower-paid work temporarily, but your visa won't reflect this (your visa locks you to the specified employer at the visa threshold salary). Plan carefully.

Life in Ireland — what to expect

  • Weather Reality:** It rains 2-3 days weekly, even in summer. November-February is dark (sunset 4 p.m.) and drizzly. July-August is best (15-17°C, sometimes sunny). Italians often struggle with this first year. Solution: invest in proper rain gear (€60-100 for decent jacket), embrace pubs, and find indoor hobbies fast. First-winter depression is real; many expats feel it.
  • Housing Market Tips & Scams:**
  • Tips:* Use Daft.ie (the legitimate marketplace—avoid rentals listed nowhere else, huge red flag). Meet landlords in person before money changes hands. Verify they own the property (ask for deed or landlord registration number). Never wire deposits before signing a lease. Use bank transfer, not cash. Check the Rental Tenancies Board for any landlord disputes. Use Facebook groups ("Dublin Housing," "Apartments Dublin") where people report scams; landlords are named and shamed.
  • Scams:* Photos from Google Images (reverse-image search anything suspicious). Prices too good (€600 for a city-centre one-bed is fake). Landlords asking for "upfront deposit before viewing" or "wire via Western Union." Requests to move fast or pay extra to "secure" a place. If anything feels off, move on—thousands of legitimate apartments exist.
  • Where Italians Live:** South Dublin (Rathmines, Ballymun, Ranelagh) has the largest Italian expat community. Stoneybatter and Smithfield are cheaper, trendy, younger. North Dublin (Clontarf, Drumcondra) is quieter, better for families. Many Italians eventually move to Cork (cheaper, smaller, more livable) after 2-3 years in Dublin.
  • Facebook Groups & Community:**
  • Join: "Italians in Dublin," "Dublin Housing," "Expats in Ireland," "Dublin Jobs." These groups are lifelines—people share apartment tips, job leads, restaurant recommendations, and emotional support when missing Italy. Italian restaurants: "Etto," "Dada," "Graffiti" are decent but pricey. Most Italians eventually cook at home (Italian food is expensive in restaurants). Buy Italian products from "Donnybrook Fair" or import from Italy via Amazon.it (shipping: €15-20, worth it for decent pasta/olive oil).
  • Cultural Surprises:**
  • Pubs are genuinely where social life happens (more than in Italy). Saying "no thanks" to pub invitations isolates you.
  • Irish are friendly but reserved initially; expect warmth after 3-4 interactions, not immediately.
  • Work-life balance is better than Italy; people leave at 5-6 p.m. and don't answer emails at night. This is good.
  • Conversation starters are weather, the Luas (tram), or football; avoid politics early on.
  • No siesta, no long lunches. Lunch is 30 minutes; dinner is 6-7 p.m. (much earlier than Italy).
  • Queuing is sacred; never cut in. People queue for buses, shops, everything.
  • Tips: 10-15% in restaurants (not expected but appreciated). Taxi drivers expect loose change.
  • Drinking culture is strong; if you don't drink, people notice but respect it. Have an answer ready ("I'm training," "I drive").
  • Transport:** Buses are the backbone (Dublin Bus, others). The Luas (tram) runs north-south and south-side. Train (Irish Rail) connects to Cork, Galway, Belfast. Dublin Bikes (public bike scheme, €45 yearly) is brilliant for city navigation. Uber exists but costs €15-25 across Dublin—taxis are sometimes cheaper. No Italian cars on road? Good—car ownership in Dublin is optional and expensive (insurance, fuel, parking).
  • Healthcare Quality:** Public system is solid for serious issues (surgery, ER, chronic disease) but slow for routine care (GP appointments: 2-3 week wait if you're not registered privately). Private is fast but expensive (€200+ per visit). HSE (public) works well; don't let people scare you. Dental and vision aren't covered—budget €200-400 yearly if you need routine care.
  • Overall Expat Happiness:** Most Italians rate Ireland 7-8 out of 10 after 2-3 years. Honeymoon phase (months 1-3) is exciting; reality phase (months 4-12) brings homesickness and frustration with weather/bureaucracy. Year 2 onwards is usually positive—you've built a life, have friends, understand the system. Main long-term complaints: weather, missing family/food, Dublin's cost of living rising, and missing Italian social culture (long meals, afternoon time with friends). Career-wise, people are typically satisfied—better pay, growth, work-life balance than Italian jobs. Most stay 3-7 years, then either recommit or move back/onward. The move is not permanent for many—it's a phase. Plan for that emotionally.
