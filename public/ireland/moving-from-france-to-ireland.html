Is it worth moving from France to Ireland?

The honest answer

Moving from France to Ireland makes sense if you want higher salaries (20-30% more in tech, finance, and healthcare), better English-speaking career mobility, and don't mind rain and higher living costs. Quality of life is genuinely good—friendly people, safe cities, strong expat community—but Dublin especially is expensive (rent comparable to Paris now), and you'll miss French healthcare, wine culture, and the Mediterranean. It suits ambitious young professionals, English speakers, and people tired of French bureaucracy. The financial reality: you'll earn more but spend more on rent and food. Skip this move if you're seeking lower costs, love French social benefits, or need family proximity—France's 35-hour work week and excellent public services are genuinely hard to beat.

Visa & permits for France citizens

  • Irish visas for French citizens: Brexit changed everything.** You're now treated like any non-EU citizen, despite historical ties. No visa needed for tourism (90 days), but working requires a permit.
  • Critical Employment Permit:** The main route for skilled workers. Salary threshold is €32,000 annually (€38,000 for non-critical skills list). Processing takes 4-8 weeks. You need a job offer first—employers must prove no Irish/EU candidate exists. Tech, healthcare, and engineering are usually approved quickly; hospitality takes longer.
  • Critical Skills Employment Permit:** Same salary threshold but faster (2-4 weeks) if you're in shortage areas: software developers, nurses, engineers, accountants. This is most French people's path.
  • Intra-Company Transfer:** If working for a multinational with Irish office, you can transfer. Easier than proving no local candidate exists.
  • Self-Employment/Start-up Visa:** Less common but possible if you're investing €50,000+ in your business.
  • Family reunification:** Non-working spouses and children under 18 can join permit holders. No separate visa needed once your permit is approved—they're dependent on your employment.
  • Irish Residence Permit (IRP):** Once approved, you get a stamp in your passport at Dublin airport immigration. This isn't a traditional visa but proof of permission to work. Valid 1-2 years, renewable.
  • No bilateral agreements exist** post-Brexit. You're starting fresh legally, despite being culturally European. Processing can be slower than pre-2020.

Before you leave France

**Get official French documents certified:** Obtain certified copies of your birth certificate, marriage certificate (if applicable), and education diplomas from your local mairie or relevant authorities—Ireland's Revenue and immigration will ask for these, and French originals aren't always accepted. **Notify French tax authorities:** File a final tax return (declaration d'impôts) and declare you're leaving France; if you leave mid-year, you're taxed only on income earned until departure. **Close or downsize French utilities and services:** Cancel or suspend gas, electricity, internet, and phone contracts (typically 1 month's notice); check for cancellation fees in your lease. **Transfer your French address officially:** Update your address with your bank, insurance company, and any French subscriptions; you'll need a French contact address for tax purposes for several years. **Get driving licence translations:** Have your French driving licence officially translated (available through Irish motor organizations) or exchange it for an Irish one upon arrival—both are valid in Ireland, but exchange is often easier. **Secure health records:** Request copies of your medical records, prescriptions, and vaccination history from your French GP in English or with certified translation; Irish GPs won't have access to French systems. **Arrange temporary Irish accommodation before arriving:** Book an Airbnb or hostel for at least 1-2 weeks; don't arrive without a place, as viewings happen fast and landlords demand immediate decisions. **Budget €3,000-5,000 for first month:** Arrange transfer to an Irish bank account you've opened remotely; France doesn't use Wise or PayPal as smoothly as UK/Ireland do, so plan ahead. **Research your job offer salary carefully:** Ask the Irish employer exactly what your take-home pay will be after tax and PRSI (social insurance)—Irish taxes are higher than France's on mid-range salaries. **Get international moving quotes:** If bringing furniture or large items, contact French movers now; shipping household goods to Ireland from France costs €2,500-6,000 depending on volume and takes 3-4 weeks.

First week in Ireland

  • Day 1 - Airport to basic setup:** Arrive, collect baggage, exit into arrivals hall. Walk 50 metres to the mobile phone kiosks (Vodafone, Three, Eir booths); buy a Three or Vodafone pay-as-you-go SIM card (€10 with €5 credit) and activate immediately. Text a photo of your Irish address and a contactable number to your temporary landlord to confirm arrival. At the airport Tesco Express, buy a 1-3 journey Leap Card (€5 refundable card + €10 credit) from the machine near the till—this works on buses, trams, and trains across Dublin. Take the Aircoach bus (€7 single, €12 return) or Luas tram (€2.10 with Leap Card) into city centre; aim for your temporary accommodation by early evening. Walk immediately to your nearest convenience store (Tesco, Dunnes, Supervalu) and buy basics: bread, milk, tea, coffee, toiletries—Irish prices are 15-25% higher than France.
  • Day 2-3 - Apartment hunting and temporary setup:** Spend full days viewing apartments on Daft.ie, SpareRoom.ie, and Facebook groups ("Irish property for rent," "Dublin apartments"). Bring €500-1,000 cash for deposit if you find something. Landlords typically demand: 4-6 weeks' rent as deposit + 1 month's rent upfront + photo ID + proof of employment (email from employer is enough). Avoid Airbnb beyond 2-3 weeks; it's €50-80/night and expensive long-term. Stay in a hostel (€25-35/night) if apartment search extends past day 3. By end of day 3, aim to have signed a lease (usually 1-year minimum, some 6-month options). Many apartments come unfurnished or semi-furnished; factor in €500 for basic furniture from Argos, Ikea, or Facebook Marketplace.
  • Day 4-5 - Registration and ID setup:** Visit your local Garda Síochána (police station) to register as a non-EU resident; bring your employment permit letter, passport, proof of address (landlord letter or utility bill—call your landlord to email one), and €50. Processing takes 30 minutes, gives you permission to work and a PPS number application form. Go immediately to your local Citizens Information Centre (search "Dublin Citizens Information Centre") with that form and your documents; they'll fast-track your PPS number (Personal Public Service number—Ireland's tax/social ID). PPS takes 2-4 weeks by post, but you'll get a temporary number to open a bank account. Register your address with Revenue online (revenue.ie) if you have PPS already, or use the temporary number.
  • Day 6-7 - Healthcare and settling in:** Register with a local GP by phone (search "GP clinic Dublin [your area]"); bring your passport, proof of address, and vaccination records. Book an appointment for day 10-14. Visit your nearest Dunnes Stores or Tesco supermarket on day 6; buy your weekly groceries and get familiar with Irish brands and prices. On day 7, explore your local area on foot: find the nearest bus stop, coffee shops, restaurants, pharmacy (Boots, Lloyds), and post office. Walk into your local pub and sit for a drink (pint of Guinness €5.50, coffee €2.50); Irish people are genuinely friendly and will chat. Buy a local newspaper (Irish Times, Irish Independent) or visit their websites to understand local news. Join the Facebook group "Expats in Dublin" or "French people in Ireland" and post an introduction—you'll be invited to drinks within days.

First month essentials

  • 1) Personal Public Service Number (PPS) and tax registration:** Your PPS number is Ireland's equivalent to France's numéro de sécurité sociale—you need it for work, healthcare, and taxes. You'll get a temporary PPS at the Garda station on day 4-5, and the permanent plastic card arrives 2-4 weeks later by post. Bring passport, proof of address (landlord letter or utility bill), and employment letter to the Citizens Information Centre; it's free and takes 20 minutes. Once you have the permanent number, register with Revenue (Ireland's tax authority) at revenue.ie—you can do this online in 10 minutes. They'll assign you a tax file number and send correspondence to your Irish address.
  • 2) Opening an Irish bank account:** Most French people use Bank of Ireland, Ulster Bank, or AIB (all have English-speaking staff, though AIB is fastest for non-residents). You'll need: passport, proof of address (landlord letter counts), employment letter, and your temporary PPS number. Processing takes 3-5 days; you'll get a debit card in 7-10 days and online banking activated immediately. Monthly fees are €0-5 depending on account type (most current accounts are free). Open the account in person at a branch—phone/video application takes longer. Set up standing orders to send money back to France: Wise.com charges 0.6-1% fee and is fastest (1-2 days); your bank's international transfer costs €15-25 per transaction and takes 3-5 days.
  • 3) Healthcare registration:** Ireland has public healthcare (HSE) and private options. As a worker, you'll pay PRSI (social insurance) automatically from salary—this covers public GP visits (free to employed people), hospital treatment, and prescriptions (€3.50 each, capped at €160/month). Register with a GP within your first 2 weeks by calling your nearest clinic and providing passport, proof of address, and vaccination records. First appointment takes 2-3 weeks to book. If you need urgent care before then, visit an out-of-hours GP clinic (€60-80 walk-in fee) or urgent care centre. Private insurance (Laya Healthcare, VHI, Irish Life Health) costs €80-150/month but means faster appointments—many expats get it for peace of mind. Get travel insurance (€10-15/month through Irish provider) to cover France visits until you establish residency.
  • 4) Driving licence transfer:** Your French driving licence is legal in Ireland indefinitely, but many expats exchange it for an Irish one for simplicity. Download the paper form or apply online at ndls.ie (National Driver Licence Service). You'll need: French licence, passport, proof of address, and €55 fee. Processing takes 4-6 weeks; you get a temporary paper licence while waiting. Irish licences last 10 years (vs. France's 15). If stopped by police, either licence is fine—exchange is optional but recommended if staying 2+ years. Keep your French licence as backup. Car insurance is mandatory (€800-1,500/year from Allianz, AXA, or local brokers); bring your French no-claims bonus letter as proof of clean driving record to reduce premiums.
  • 5) France-specific professional qualification recognition:** If you're a doctor, nurse, accountant, lawyer, or engineer, Ireland has fast-track recognition for EU qualifications through regulated bodies (Medical Council, Nursing and Midwifery Board, CPA, Law Society). Contact the relevant body within your first week; most recognition is automatic, but some professions require 2-4 weeks administrative processing and small registration fees (€50-200). Non-regulated professions (software development, marketing, HR) don't need recognition—your French experience is valued as-is. Request certified English copies of your French diplomas and professional references before leaving France.

Money & cost of living in Ireland

  • Monthly budget for a single person in Dublin (2024 prices):**
  • Tight budget (student/entry-level, €1,650/month):** Rent €600 (shared house, southside or north Dublin suburbs), groceries €150, utilities €60, phone €15, transport pass €90, eating out €250 (pub twice weekly), entertainment €100, health insurance €0 (covered by employer PRSI), clothes/misc €200. This assumes you're earning €28,000-32,000 gross (€1,950 net monthly after tax and PRSI). You're saving €200/month but cutting corners.
  • Comfortable budget (mid-career, €2,400/month):** Rent €850 (1-bed flat, city centre fringe like Rathmines, Stoneybatter), groceries €200, utilities €90, phone €15, transport €90, eating out €450 (restaurants 2-3x weekly), entertainment €200 (gym, cinema, drinks), health insurance €100 (private, optional but nice), clothes €200, misc €305. Assumes €42,000-50,000 gross (€2,850 net monthly). You're saving €450/month and living well.
  • Good lifestyle (€3,200/month):** Rent €1,100 (nice 1-bed, central Dublin 2/4/6), groceries €250, utilities €120, phone €20, transport €90, eating out €700 (restaurants/brunches regularly), entertainment €350 (gym, activities, weekends away), health insurance €150 (private top-up), clothes €300, misc €320. Assumes €55,000+ gross (€3,600+ net). You're saving €400/month and barely thinking about money.
  • Reality check:** Dublin rent rose 30% in 2023-2024; a city-centre 1-bed flat now costs €1,000-1,300. Suburbs (Ballymun, Tallaght) are €700-850 but add 45 minutes commute. Groceries cost 20% more than France—a Carrefour basket that costs €40 in France costs €50 in Tesco Dublin. Eating out is expensive: lunch €12-15, dinner €25-40. Irish salaries are 25-30% higher than France, but taxes are higher too (40% on earnings above €40,000 vs. France's 45% at €50,000+).
  • Sending money to France:** Wise.com is fastest and cheapest—0.6% fee, arrives in 1-2 days, no minimum transfer. Your Irish bank's international transfer costs €15-25 and takes 3-5 days. Wise is used by 90% of expats. For large transfers (€5,000+), Wise fees are negligible compared to bank rates. Irish tax authorities don't care if you send money home—it's already taxed. French authorities won't tax money you've already paid Irish tax on (no double taxation treaty applies, but France assumes you're taxed on worldwide income, so document everything).
  • Tax implications:** You're taxed in Ireland on Irish income once resident. France taxes worldwide income for French residents, but you cease being French tax-resident when you establish Irish residency (notification to French tax office needed). Keep records of your departure date and first Irish address. You may owe a final French tax in the year you leave. No major bilateral tax treaty exists post-Brexit, but the EU standard rules apply—you're taxed where you work, not where you're a citizen.

Finding work in Ireland

  • Top 5 hiring sectors in Ireland for people from France:**
  • 1) Technology/Software Development:** Salary range €55,000-120,000 (mid-level to senior). Ireland is a European tech hub—Google, Meta, Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, Stripe all have major offices in Dublin. Demand far exceeds supply; French developers are highly valued. Average time to hire: 2-4 weeks. Job sites: LinkedIn, Indeed, Stack Overflow jobs. Cultural note: very casual, flat hierarchies, flexible hours. You'll work 37.5 hours officially but often less if results-focused.
  • 2) Financial Services/Accountancy:** Salary range €42,000-90,000. Dublin is a banking hub; Big Four accountancies (Deloitte, PWC, KPMG, EY) hire heavily. French accounting qualifications transfer easily via regulated recognition. Average time to hire: 3-6 weeks (more formal interview process than tech). Job sites: LinkedIn, target firms' careers pages, IrishJobs.ie.
  • 3) Healthcare/Nursing:** Salary range €32,000-55,000 (nurses), €55,000-95,000 (doctors). Ireland has chronic healthcare worker shortages. EU medical and nursing qualifications recognized fast (2-4 weeks) through the Medical Council or Nursing and Midwifery Board of Ireland. French nurses especially valued. Average time to hire: 1-2 weeks. Job sites: HSE.ie (public), private hospital websites, Indeed.
  • 4) Sales/Business Development:** Salary range €35,000-70,000 + commission. Tech companies, pharma, and multinationals hire heavily. English fluency is essential; French language skills are bonus. Less formal recognition needed—sales is sales everywhere. Average time to hire: 2-4 weeks. Job sites: LinkedIn, Indeed, specific company sites.
  • 5) Education/Language teaching:** Salary range €28,000-45,000. French language teachers are wanted in secondary schools and language centres. Teaching qualifications (CAPES, Agrégation) transfer; TEFL (Teaching English as Foreign Language) sometimes required if you want to teach English instead. Average time to hire: 2-8 weeks (hiring cycles align with academic years—August and January are busy). Job sites: TeachingEnglish.org.uk, specific school websites, Indeed.
  • Qualification recognition:** Science, engineering, and regulated professions (law, medicine, accounting) go through formal bodies and take 2-4 weeks with small fees (€50-200). Non-regulated fields don't need recognition—your French CV and experience matter. French graduates are seen as well-educated; the Grandes Écoles reputation carries weight.
  • Language requirements:** English is essential for all sectors. Virtually all workplaces communicate in English. Your French accent won't hurt you. Many tech companies have French or European staff; you'll find a community. Some finance roles value French-language skills (20-30% premium for bilingual candidates).
  • After-tax salary expectations:** €50,000 gross → €3,300 net/month. €65,000 gross → €4,100 net/month. €85,000 gross → €5,000 net/month. Irish income tax is 20% up to €40,000, then 40% above that, plus 10.75% PRSI (social insurance). French taxes were lower on mid-range salaries, but Irish salaries are higher, so most people come out ahead financially.
  • Best job sites:** LinkedIn (most active, international), Indeed.ie (Irish-specific), IrishJobs.ie (niche roles), StackOverflow jobs (tech), CareerStructure.ie (finance). Your CV should emphasize quantified results and specific achievements—Irish hiring is results-focused. Interview culture is friendly, casual; expect 2-3 rounds of interviews and a practical test for tech roles. Salary negotiation is normal and expected—always ask for 10-15% more than their first offer.
  • France experience valued?** Yes, significantly. French companies' reputation for rigor, quality, and technical excellence is respected. If you worked for a major French tech company, bank, or engineering firm, mention it prominently—it signals reliability and discipline.

Life in Ireland — what to expect

  • Weather and climate:** Brace yourself for 200 rainy days yearly. Dublin's climate is mild (5-15°C winter, 15-20°C summer) but perpetually grey. Bring quality rain gear—the Irish spend €100-200 on good jackets yearly. Winter is dark (sunset at 4pm in December). This affects mental health for some French expats; vitamin D supplements are common. You'll never have the Mediterranean sunshine you're used to. Positives: no snow, no extreme heat, gardens stay green year-round.
  • Housing market reality and scams:** Dublin rental prices are now equivalent to Paris. A 1-bed flat costs €900-1,200/month in central areas, €700-850 in suburbs. Deposits are non-refundable if you damage the place; landlords are often aggressive about damage claims (€200 for a small wall scuff). Most contracts are 1-year minimum; breaking early costs 2 months' rent penalty. Beware scams: never pay a deposit before viewing in person and signing. Use Daft.ie and SpareRoom.ie only—Facebook Marketplace has many fraudsters. Always verify the landlord is legitimate (check property records at PropertyPriceRegister.ie). House-sharing is common for ages 20-35; single occupancy is expensive. Outside Dublin (Cork, Galway, Limerick), rent drops 30-40% and quality of life rises significantly—consider this if Dublin feels too pricey or hectic.
  • Where French people live:** Dún Laoghaire and Howth (coastal, calm, south/north Dublin edges) attract older French expats and families. Rathmines, Stoneybatter, and Smithfield appeal to young professionals (vibrant, walkable, central). Temple Bar area has Irish tourist traps and young party culture—fine for a few months, avoid long-term. Ballsbridge is expensive and corporate. North Dublin (Drumcondra, Whitehall) is cheaper and less gentrified. Cork city attracts creative French people seeking slower pace (1 hour south, 40% cheaper rent, 60,000 population). Most French expats cluster in tech companies' office areas (Google in Dublin 4, Meta in Ballymun)—commute proximity drives housing choices.
  • Community and Facebook groups:** "Expats in Dublin" (8,000+ members), "French people in Ireland" (3,000+ members), and "Dublin housing" groups are active and helpful. Monthly expat meetups happen at pubs; you'll make friends fast. Irish people are famously friendly—strike up conversations in pubs and you'll be invited to parties. French expats often form tight communities; some find this either comforting or limiting (you might miss native Irish friendships if you cluster only with French people).
  • Cultural surprises:** Irish people value small talk and humor—silence feels rude, not polite like in France. Work culture is far more casual; calling your boss by first name is normal. Religion is less visible than stereotypes suggest, though Ireland remains more Catholic culturally than France. Punctuality is taken seriously despite Ireland's "relaxed" reputation—being 15 minutes late to a meeting is noticed. Pubs are the social hub, not cafés; expect to spend €5.50 on a pint and 2+ hours there. Irish bureaucracy is less painful than French but slower—getting things done takes time and patience. Recycling is mandatory and taken seriously—violate it and neighbors will judge you. Irish food culture is improving but still behind France's; good restaurants exist but cost more. Breakfast culture is huge (full Irish breakfast €12-15); embrace it.
  • Finding French food:** Ireland has excellent French restaurants (€40-60 mains), but they're expensive and concentrated in Dublin. Supermarkets have French sections now; Marks & Spencer sells decent French cheese and wine. French restaurants cluster in Dublin 2 (Baile, L'Ecrivain, Patrick Guilbaud). Wine prices are high (€15 for a drinkable bottle, €30+ for good ones—twice French prices). Francophile delis exist in Dublin suburbs; ask locals. Many French expats cook at home and order French groceries online from Paris (Ocado, Auchan Express) for birthday celebrations—costs €20-30 shipping plus product cost, so saved for special occasions.
  • Transport:** Dublin's public transport (buses, trams) is decent but expensive compared to France—€90/month for unlimited access vs. €45 in Paris. A car is not necessary in Dublin city centre but useful for Cork or Galway. Irish petrol costs €1.30/litre (vs. €1.50-1.80 in France, so actually slightly cheaper now). Driving is on the left; infrastructure is fine but road rage is real. Cycling is growing but Dublin has fewer safe bike lanes than European cities. Walking is pleasant; most neighborhoods are walkable.
  • Healthcare quality:** Ireland's public healthcare is competent but slower than France's. Wait times for specialists are 3-6 months (vs. weeks in France). GPs are good but appointment-dependent; walk-in clinics don't exist. Prescriptions are €3.50 each (cheap vs. France). Private healthcare is expensive (€150-300/visit) but faster. Overall, standards are high but the system is stretched. Irish healthcare is less "free" than French—you pay for GP visits indirectly through PRSI payroll tax, and there's a perception of less preventive focus.
  • Overall expat happiness:** Most French expats in Ireland are satisfied, especially after 6-12 months. The financial boost, English-language career opportunities, and friendly culture offset weather and homesickness. Year 1 is often tough (rainy, lonely, culture shock); year 2 onwards improves dramatically as you build friendships and routines. People with Irish heritage or English fluency adapt faster. Those deeply attached to French food, weather, or social systems struggle more. Common regrets: underestimating Dublin's expense, not learning Irish pub culture sooner, missing French healthcare speed. Common loves: salary increases, work-life balance, expat community friendliness, Ireland's natural beauty (cliffs, coastline), pub culture once you embrace it. Most French expats stay 3-5 years; many extend indefinitely once they buy property or establish families.
