Is it worth moving from Poland to Ireland?

The honest answer

Moving from Poland to Ireland is genuinely worth it if you're after better English-language job prospects and don't mind paying 40% more for rent and groceries. The quality of life is high—good healthcare, safe cities, excellent pubs culture—but wages don't match Dublin's cost of living the way they used to. You'll earn €35,000–€50,000 starting out in tech or professional roles (versus €20,000–€30,000 in Poland), but a city-centre apartment costs €1,200–€1,600 monthly versus €600–€900 in Warsaw. This move suits people seeking career advancement, English immersion, or fresh starts; it's tough if you're sending money home or have family depending on Polish-level salaries. The real win is career building and stability, not getting rich.

Visa & permits for Poland citizens

  • The Good News:** Polish citizens don't need a visa to visit Ireland—you get 90 days visa-free as an EU citizen under the Common Travel Area, even after Brexit. This is unique to Ireland-UK-Poland relations and gives you time to job-hunt.
  • Work Permit Routes:**
  • Employment Permit* is the main route. Your employer must sponsor you, proving no Irish/EU candidate exists. Processing takes 4–8 weeks; salary thresholds vary by role (tech: €32,000+, nursing: €30,000+, general: €28,000+). Cost is €1,000–€500 depending on salary. Once approved, you get 2 years; renewable.
  • Critical Skills Employment Permit* is faster (2–4 weeks) for roles on Ireland's shortage list: software engineers, nurses, architects. Salary thresholds higher (€64,000+), but processing is quicker and you have more employer flexibility.
  • Intra-Company Transfer* if your Polish employer has an Irish branch—company handles it, usually smoother.
  • Family/Partner Routes:** Spouses of Irish/EU citizens qualify for residence rights. Partners (unmarried) need to prove 2 years cohabitation and financial dependency; processing 8–12 weeks.
  • Student Route:** Student visa valid for course duration plus 4 months post-graduation work. Tuition: €10,000–€20,000/year for non-EU (yes, Poland is non-EU now).
  • No Bilateral Agreement:** Poland and Ireland don't have special work agreements, so standard permits apply. Unlike some EU countries, there's no "points-based" fast track for Polish workers.

Before you leave Poland

  • 1. Get official copies of qualifications certified:** If you're an engineer, teacher, nurse, or accountant, obtain Polish-authenticated copies of diplomas and transcripts in English translation—Ireland will ask. Contact your university or use a certified translator; costs €50–€150 total. Do this now; it takes 2–3 weeks and you'll need it for work permits or professional registration.
  • 2. Collect employment history and references in writing:** Ask your current Polish employer for a formal letter on company letterhead stating your role, dates, and performance. Irish employers and permit officers want this; it's rare in Poland but standard there. Get 2–3 references you can contact; emails work but phone-callable referees are better.
  • 3. Notify Polish tax office (Urząd Skarbowy) of your move:** File a departure notice or they may assume you're still taxable in Poland. Takes one visit; free. You'll pay no Polish tax once employed in Ireland (tax residency transfers), but get it documented.
  • 4. Check your bank account access abroad:** Contact your Polish bank and ask if your card/app works in Ireland. Many do, but some require notification. Arrange a small test transfer to ensure no blocks. You'll want Polish account access for a few months.
  • 5. Secure rental reference from Polish landlord or previous employer:** Get a written letter in English stating you paid rent on time and caused no damage. Irish landlords request this; Polish rental history is unfamiliar to them, so documentation helps credibility.
  • 6. Book accommodation visits or arrange agent viewings:** Don't commit to a lease unseen. Use Daft.ie to identify properties, email landlords, book video tours or arrange an agent. Budget €200–€500 for viewing trips if possible, or plan 1–2 weeks of Airbnb on arrival to apartment-hunt in person.
  • 7. Arrange Polish health insurance bridge or private cover:** Your EHIC card works in Ireland for emergencies, but registering with Irish GP takes 2–4 weeks. Buy 1–2 months private health insurance (€40–€80/month) to cover any medical needs. Websites like Laya Healthcare or VHI offer quick online policies.
  • 8. Set aside €3,000–€4,000 arrival funds in euros or pounds:** First month covers deposit (often €1,500–€2,000), first rent, SIM card, transport, groceries. Bring partly in cash (€1,500) and partly as accessible funds (Wise transfer). Avoid bringing more than €10,000 cash—nothing illegal, but declaring is tedious.
  • 9. Arrange transport from airport:** Book a rental car (€200–€300/week) if moving to countryside, or pre-arrange a taxi/bus ticket (€10–€20). Irish taxis from airport are pricey (€25–€40); Aircoach bus is €7–€12. Sorted transport saves stress on arrival day.
  • 10. Create a job-search prep folder:** Tailor your CV to Irish format (one page, no photo, no age/marital status). Write a generic cover letter template. Research 10–15 companies you'd target. Have these ready to send within days of arrival; you'll have momentum and a visa-free 90 days to job-hunt.

First week in Ireland

  • Day 1 (Arrival):**
  • Touch down and head straight to the airport SIM card stand—buy a Three or Vodafone prepaid SIM (€20 gets you credit + data). Takes 10 minutes. Then buy an Leap card (€5 deposit) from the transport desk; touch it on any Dublin bus and you've got transport sorted. If you're in Dublin, take the Aircoach bus to city centre (€12, 30 mins) or a taxi if tired. Check into your pre-booked Airbnb or budget hotel (€40–€70/night). Your first evening: walk your neighbourhood, find a supermarket, buy basics (milk, bread, coffee—budget €30). Sleep well; jet lag hits less hard than you'd expect from Poland.
  • Days 2–3 (Apartment Hunting):**
  • Now the real work starts. Open Daft.ie, MyHome.ie, and Rent.ie on your laptop. Set alerts for your budget area (Southside Dublin is pricier—€1,400+; Northside or suburbs €900–€1,200). Message 5–10 landlords immediately; most reply within hours. Arrange viewings for Days 3–4. Visit properties in person—photos lie. Ask about: lease length (6 months minimum, usually 12), when you can move in, deposit amount, utilities included. Budget €1,500–€2,000 for a deposit (refundable) plus first month rent upfront. Bring a folder with your employment letter, Polish landlord reference, and ID; landlords want proof you'll pay. Expect to decide within 24 hours or lose the flat—supply is tight.
  • Days 4–5 (Register with Authorities):**
  • Once you have accommodation, register with An Garda Síochána (Irish police) if staying longer than 3 months—this is free and takes 30 minutes. Find your local Garda station; bring passport, rental agreement, and proof of address. You'll get a registration number (not essential but useful). Visit your local council office (Dublin City Council, etc.) to register for property tax purposes—again, free, 20 minutes. Neither is urgent, but both are better done early. They confirm your residency for other applications.
  • Days 6–7 (GP & Orientation):**
  • Find a GP practice near your home—search "GP near me" on Healthlink.ie or ask your landlord for recommendations. Ring and ask to register; first appointment often takes 2–4 weeks, so book immediately. Tell them you're a new resident without Irish health history; they'll take your details. Buy a weekly travel pass (Leap card, €35 for unlimited bus/tram/train) and explore your neighbourhood properly. Visit a supermarket, pharmacy, and local pub. Stop into the library—Dublin libraries are excellent, free, and good for WiFi if your Airbnb is dodgy. By Day 7, you should feel anchored: accommodation sorted, GP registered, transport working, and basic orientation done.

First month essentials

  • 1. Personal Public Service Number (PPS) & Tax:**
  • Your PPS number is essential for employment and taxation. Go to your local Department of Enterprise, Trade and Employment office (Dublin has several) or any post office offering the service. Bring: passport, rental agreement, and a proof of address (utility bill takes weeks, so a landlord letter works). The process takes 15 minutes; you'll get a temporary number immediately, official card in 2–3 weeks. Cost: free. Without this, employers won't process payroll. Do this in Week 1; it's the bottleneck for getting paid.
  • 2. Bank Account:**
  • Open a bank account by Week 2—don't delay. Best banks for newcomers: Bank of Ireland and AIB are big, have branches everywhere, and accept non-residents easily. You'll need: passport, proof of address (rental agreement), PPS number (temporary is fine), and €100 minimum deposit. Online banks like N26 or Revolut are faster (15 minutes) but Ireland's payroll system favors traditional banks. Processing takes 5–10 working days; you'll get a debit card in 2–3 weeks. Cost: free (no monthly fee for basic accounts). Use this account for salary deposits and bills—it establishes financial history in Ireland, useful for mortgages later.
  • 3. Healthcare Registration:**
  • You've already booked a GP; now follow up. Once registered, you're eligible for public healthcare (free GP visits if you earn under €18,500; otherwise €50–€60 per visit). Prescription costs €12.50 each regardless of actual drug cost (capped). Private health insurance is optional but recommended (€50–€100/month for basic cover); Laya Healthcare and VHI are largest. You won't get a public dentist easily—budget €100–€200 for private dental work. Emergency hospital care is free. If you're not registered with a GP and need urgent care, go to an A&E (ER equivalent) but be prepared to wait 3–5 hours; it's NHS-style but slower. Register with GP by Week 2; first appointment usually by Week 4.
  • 4. Driving Licence Transfer:**
  • If you have a Polish driving licence, you can drive in Ireland for one year on it. After that, transfer to an Irish licence. Go to the NDLS (National Driver Licence Service) office or post office offering the service with: Polish licence, passport, proof of address, PPS number, and €55 fee. Processing takes 4–6 weeks by post; they'll issue an Irish plastic card. If you need to drive immediately (rental car won't let you with Polish licence for long), apply urgently—costs extra €10 but processed in 2–3 weeks. Car insurance is mandatory (€400–€800/year for first-time Irish drivers); avoid buying a car until you've sorted a licence and opened a bank account.
  • 5. Qualification Recognition (If Professional):**
  • If you're an engineer, nurse, teacher, accountant, or architect, you need professional recognition for regulated work. Engineers contact Engineers Ireland; nurses contact the Nursing and Midwifery Board of Ireland (NMBI); teachers contact the Department of Education. Bring your diplomas (English-translated copies), employment history, and PPS number. Processing: 2–12 weeks depending on profession. Cost: €100–€300. Some professions (IT, marketing) don't need official recognition—just mention qualifications on CVs. This can hold up job offers, so if you're in a regulated field, start applications in Week 1. Once approved, you're gold for hiring.

Money & cost of living in Ireland

  • Monthly Budget—Single Person, Dublin:**
  • Rent:* City centre (Southside, Temple Bar area): €1,500–€1,800/month for a one-bed; Northside or suburbs (Ballymun, Tallaght): €900–€1,200. Budget €1,200 as baseline.
  • Groceries:* €250–€300/month. Irish supermarkets (Tesco, Dunnes, Lidl) are cheaper than you'd expect if you avoid premium brands. A coffee is €2.50, lunch sandwich €5, dinner out €15–€25.
  • Utilities (Electricity, Gas, Water):* €100–€150/month combined. Winter heating costs more; summer less. Water and bins often included in rent.
  • Transport:* Weekly Leap card pass €35 (unlimited bus/tram). Monthly: €140. If you own a car, add €400–€600 for insurance, fuel, and maintenance.
  • Phone:* €15–€30/month (Three, Vodafone, Virgin Media). Unlimited data plans common.
  • Eating Out & Entertainment:* €200–€300/month if you socialize twice weekly. Pint of beer: €6–€7. Cinema: €12. Gym: €30–€50.
  • Health Insurance (Private):* €50–€100/month optional but recommended.
  • Other (Clothing, Haircuts, etc.):* €100–€150/month.
  • Total Tight Budget:** €2,100–€2,400/month (city centre, minimal eating out).
  • Total Comfortable Budget:** €2,800–€3,200/month (good flat, regular social life, occasional travel).
  • Total Good Lifestyle:** €3,500–€4,500/month (nice flat, eating out regularly, hobbies, savings).
  • Sending Money to Poland:**
  • Use Wise (formerly TransferWise): 1–2% fee, mid-market exchange rate, arrives in 24 hours. Transfer €500–€1,000 at a time. Avoid bank transfers (5–8% fees). Wise is trusted, transparent, and faster than PayPal. Cost example: sending €1,000 costs roughly €10–€15 in fees.
  • Tax & Money Considerations:**
  • Ireland taxes income at 20–40% depending on income bracket (standard rate €20% up to €40,000, then 40%). As a Polish resident working in Ireland, you pay Irish tax on Irish income. No bilateral tax treaty reduces this, but it's straightforward—your employer handles PAYE (Pay As You Earn). If you send money to Poland and it's personal savings, no tax implication. If you work for a Polish company while based in Ireland, it's complicated—consult an accountant (€200–€400 for initial advice).
  • Salary Reality Check:**
  • Graduate tech roles: €35,000–€42,000 (Dublin). Experienced tech (5+ years): €55,000–€75,000. Nursing: €32,000–€40,000. Teaching: €33,000–€45,000 depending on qualification. Construction trades: €40,000–€60,000. Customer service: €26,000–€32,000. After tax and PRSI (social insurance ~4%), a €40,000 gross salary nets roughly €30,000–€31,000 annually, or €2,500/month. Tight but workable in Dublin if you're in shared housing initially.

Finding work in Ireland

  • Top 5 Hiring Sectors:**
  • 1. **Tech/Software Development:** Highest demand. Dublin has 300+ tech companies (Google, Facebook, LinkedIn, Stripe, etc.). Salaries: €40,000–€85,000 starting; €80,000–€150,000+ senior. They value EU experience. Interview culture: technical assessments, behavioural questions, remote-friendly.
  • 2. **Healthcare/Nursing:** Critical shortage. Hospitals and private clinics hire constantly. Salaries: €32,000–€42,000 (nurses), €50,000+ (doctors, specialists). Your Polish qualification must be recognized by NMBI; process takes 4–8 weeks.
  • 3. **Construction/Trades:** Electricians, plumbers, carpenters earning €40,000–€65,000. Qualifications recognized if EU-approved. Less language-dependent. Job sites: irishjobs.ie, findajob.ie.
  • 4. **Finance/Accounting:** Dublin is an EU financial hub. Salaries: €35,000–€65,000 starting; qualification (ACCA, CPA) helps. Language: fluent English required.
  • 5. **Customer Service/Sales:** Call centres in Dublin hire year-round. Salaries: €24,000–€32,000. Your Polish language is a bonus (supporting Polish clients). Easier entry point if you're job-hunting urgently.
  • Best Job Sites:** LinkedIn (most professional roles), irishjobs.ie (broad), findajob.ie (construction/trades), jobs.ie (everything), Reed.ie (recruitment agencies), Indeed.ie.
  • Qualification Recognition:** This is critical and slow. Engineers: contact Engineers Ireland (€150 fee, 6–12 weeks). Teachers: contact Department of Education (€300, 8 weeks). Nurses: NMBI (€100, 4–8 weeks). Accountants: Chartered Accountants Ireland (€200, 6–10 weeks). Most other qualifications don't need formal recognition—just CV them.
  • Language & Culture:** Interview culture is less formal than UK but more so than Poland. Expect: behavioural questions ("Tell me about a time you led a project"), technical tests (if tech), and second-round panels. You'll interview better if you research the company and ask thoughtful questions. Polish accent is fine; English fluency essential.
  • After-Tax Reality:** A €45,000 tech job nets €33,500/year or €2,790/month after tax and social insurance. Enough for a decent life in Dublin but not luxury. Senior roles (€70,000+) net €48,000+/year—that's comfortable, savings-possible money.
  • Poland Experience Valued:** Yes, mostly. EU work experience translates well. Polish tech workers are respected. Polish nurses fill shortages. Be specific: "Led 8-person team at Polish fintech" beats "worked in Poland."
  • Timeline:** Job-hunting takes 4–8 weeks from application to offer. Start searching before arrival if possible. Most employers will sponsor an employment permit if you're a good fit (they cover costs). Salary negotiation: ask for €3,000–€5,000 more than their first offer—it often works. Benefits: healthcare, pension (mandatory employer contribution 3%), holidays (20 days minimum).

Life in Ireland — what to expect

  • Weather:** Cold, wet, grey. October–March is 5–10°C, drizzly, dark by 4 PM. July–August is 16–20°C, pleasant, bright until 9 PM. Bring waterproof jacket, umbrella, good boots. You'll hear "Ah, it's a bit damp today" constantly as small talk. You adapt quickly. It's not Poland's freezing winters, but it's depressing if you love sunshine.
  • Housing Market Reality & Scams:**
  • Dublin rental market is tight and overpriced. Average one-bed: €1,200–€1,600/month. Red flags: landlord asking for deposit before viewing, rent prices drastically below market (likely Airbnb), no written lease offered. Legitimate landlords provide: written lease signed by both parties, deposit lodged with a licensed scheme (Threshold or equivalent), rent-setting form. Avoid: "cash only," deposits sent abroad, no contract. Use only Daft.ie, MyHome.ie, and Rent.ie; Facebook Marketplace and Airbnb are flooded with scams. Many landlords discriminate against non-Irish; if rejected without reason, it might be bias (document it).
  • Where Poles Tend to Live:**
  • Ballymun (North Dublin): large Polish community, cheaper (€900–€1,100), diverse, but rougher area. Phibsborough/Stoneybatter (Northside): younger professionals, Polish cafes, €1,200–€1,400. Tallaght/Jobstown (Southside): families, cheaper, suburban, 40 mins to city. Many Poles also live central (Temple Bar, South City Centre) if they're working in tech and earning well. Join "Poles in Dublin" Facebook groups—they post flat shares, jobs, advice constantly. Groups like "Polish Community in Ireland" (3,000+ members) are goldmines for orientation.
  • Cultural Surprises:**
  • 1. Pub culture is real and expected. Friday after work, your colleagues invite you for a pint. Not going occasionally seems rude. Pints are social glue.
  • 2. Irish people are indirect—"That's grand" means "no thanks." "We'll see" means no.
  • 3. Bureaucracy is slower than Poland. Same-day results are rare; 4–6 weeks is normal.
  • 4. Work-life balance is better. Leaving at 5 PM is normal; staying late is questioned.
  • 5. Tipping is expected (10–15% in restaurants), unlike Poland.
  • 6. Landlords are less reliable than Polish ones—enforcement is loose; issues take months to resolve.
  • 7. Irish people complain about weather constantly; you'll join in.
  • Finding Polish Food:**
  • Lidl and Tesco stock Polish basics (żurek, piekarnik, zakuski). Dedicated Polish shops in Phibsborough (Baltic Deli, Paczki Palace) sell proper food—piernik, oscypek, good butter. Expect to pay 20–30% more than Poland. Restaurants: few authentic Polish places, but Irish-Polish fusion spots are growing (Collie's Barmkitchen in Temple Bar). You'll miss proper Polish food; budget monthly Polish grocery hauls or ship packages from Poland (DHL costs €30–€50).
  • Transport:**
  • Dublin bus system (Dublin Bus, Luas tram) is decent but slow. Leap card makes it simple. Cycling is popular; rent a bike (Bleeper Bikes, €5–€10/month) or buy one (€200–€400). Driving in Dublin is frustrating—traffic, expensive parking (€3/hour city centre), aggressive drivers. If outside Dublin, a car is essential (Cork, Galway, Limerick). Trains (Irish Rail) are reliable but pricey for long journeys. Flying to London (€30–€50 with Ryanair) is cheaper than rail to Galway.
  • Healthcare Quality:**
  • Public system (free GP care under €18,500 income, then €50/visit; free hospital A&E) works but is slow. Wait times: GP appointments 1–2 weeks, hospital outpatient 3–6 weeks. Private care is faster but expensive (€150–€300 per GP visit, €2,000+ for specialists). Prescriptions capped at €12.50 is brilliant. Emergency care is good—ambulance quick, hospitals competent. Private insurance (Laya, VHI, Aviva) costs €50–€150/month and covers GP visits, scans, and some hospital beds. Overall: better than you'd expect, slower than Poland for non-urgent issues.
  • Expat Happiness:**
  • Most Poles who move to Ireland are happy within 6 months. Complaints: rent costs, grey weather, slower bureaucracy, homesickness for family. Wins: better salaries than Poland, English immersion, career growth, friendly expat communities, pubs, safety. People who struggle: those deeply rooted in Poland, families missing children, those expecting quick wealth. People who thrive: career-builders, language learners, adventurous types, remote workers benefiting from Euro salary.
  • Community Tips:**
  • Join: "Poles in Dublin," "Polish Community in Ireland," "Expats in Dublin," "Irish Tech Community" (if tech). Facebook is where it happens. Attend Polish Independence Day events (November), St. Andrzej celebrations (November), summer Polish festivals. Most major Irish cities have Polish communities; Cork and Galway are smaller but friendly. Finding your people takes 2–3 months; patience pays off. After a year, many say Ireland feels like home.
