Is it worth moving from Portugal to Ireland?

The honest answer

Moving from Portugal to Ireland makes sense if you're chasing higher salaries—tech and finance jobs pay 30-50% more here—and don't mind rain, crowds, and cold. Your quality of life depends entirely on what matters to you: Portugal wins on weather, cost of living, and lifestyle pace; Ireland wins on career growth, wages, and opportunities. Dublin especially feels expensive and hectic compared to most Portuguese cities, but outside Dublin (Cork, Galway, Limerick) life is cheaper and calmer. This move suits ambitious professionals, young families seeking better schooling systems, and people who want to build savings fast. Be honest: if you move for money, you'll earn it; if you move expecting Portugal with better jobs, you'll be disappointed.

Visa & permits for Portugal citizens

  • As a Portuguese citizen, you have freedom of movement within the EU and don't need a visa to enter Ireland. You can stay visa-free for up to three months as a tourist. However, to live and work long-term, you need to register with Irish immigration authorities.
  • Work Visa/Employment Permit:** If you have a job offer, your employer can sponsor an Employment Permit. Most common is the Critical Skills Employment Permit for roles paying €32,000+ annually (€64,000+ in Dublin), processed in 4-8 weeks. Tech, healthcare, and skilled trades are fast-tracked. No permit needed if you earn over €64,000 in Dublin or €32,000 elsewhere—you're considered highly skilled.
  • Startup/Self-Employment:** If you're starting a business, you can apply for a self-employment visa; you'll need €10,000 proof of funds and a viable business plan. Processing takes 8-12 weeks.
  • Family/Partner Route:** If married or in a civil partnership with an Irish/EU citizen, you register as a family member and get automatic residence rights. Partners of non-EEA citizens need separate sponsorship.
  • Student Route:** Full-time students get automatic residence; post-study work permit allows 12-24 months of work depending on qualification level.
  • Registration:** Within three months of arrival, register with the Irish Naturalisation and Immigration Service (INIS) by appointment. Bring your passport, proof of employment/funds, accommodation address, and a completed form.

Before you leave Portugal

Notify your Portuguese tax authority (Autoridade Tributária) of your departure and request a departure certificate—you'll need this to prove non-residency status. Settle any outstanding Portuguese tax or social security contributions; being clean on this matters for future visits and inheritance issues. Cancel or transfer your Portuguese health insurance; get a European Health Insurance Card (EHIC) from your local health centre—it covers emergency care in Ireland while you're settling in. Close or downgrade Portuguese utilities (electricity, water, internet, mobile) and check meter readings carefully to avoid final bills. Cancel any subscriptions tied to Portuguese accounts or addresses. If you own property, decide whether to rent it out (requires tax registration as a landlord) or sell; rental income is taxable in both countries. If you're leasing a car, confirm you can transfer it to Ireland or end the lease early; many leases charge penalties. Gather all original documents: birth certificate, marriage certificate (if applicable), educational qualifications, professional certifications, and driving licence—get these legalised by a notary if you'll need them recognised in Ireland. Open an international transfer account with Wise or Remitly before leaving; you'll need this for salary payments and sending money home without getting ripped off on exchange rates.

First week in Ireland

  • Day 1:** Land, collect luggage, head straight to the airport's mobile shop. Buy a Vodafone or Three SIM card (€20-30) with a starter package—you need connectivity immediately. Grab a Leap Card from a convenience store (€5) and load €20 credit; this covers all Dublin buses and trams. Get to your temporary accommodation (hotel, Airbnb, or hostel) and rest—jet lag from Portugal isn't severe but the light difference disorients people. Eat something familiar if possible; your body needs comfort.
  • Days 2-3:** Search accommodation seriously. Use Daft.ie (Ireland's main property site), SpareRoom.ie for flat shares, and Facebook groups like "Housing in Dublin" or your specific city. Message multiple landlords same day—good places rent within hours. Visit 3-4 places in person; see the actual area, check water pressure, ask how heating works. Budget €900-1,200 monthly for a Dublin one-bedroom (city centre) or €700-900 (suburbs). Outside Dublin: €600-800. Book immediately once you find something decent. Most landlords want references; offer your Portuguese employer's letter. Bring a folder of documents to viewings—it speeds things up.
  • Days 4-5:** Register with local authorities. Visit your local Garda station (police station) with your passport and accommodation proof to register your address—takes 20 minutes. Book an appointment with INIS online for formal residence registration; this can take 2-4 weeks. Go to Revenue (Irish tax office) and apply for a Personal Public Service Number (PPS)—this is your tax ID and you need it for everything. Bring passport, employment contract, and address proof. Process takes 2-3 weeks but they give you a temporary number immediately.
  • Days 6-7:** Register with a GP (general doctor). Visit your local medical centre or use the Health Service Executive (HSE) finder online. Bring ID, proof of address, and proof of employment. First appointment takes 30 minutes. Register with your bank—most accept non-residents with employment contracts. Download the apps for Leap Card top-ups, TfL Journey Planner, and Google Maps—Irish signage is sometimes vague. Walk around your neighbourhood; find the supermarket, pharmacy, post office, and a good coffee spot. This sounds simple but settling your nervous system matters.

First month essentials

  • 1. Personal Public Service Number (PPS):** This is your Irish tax and social ID, issued by Revenue. Visit your local Revenue office (addresses on revenue.ie) with your passport, employment contract, and accommodation proof. Bring originals and a photocopy. They issue a temporary number on the spot and post your permanent card within 2-3 weeks. Cost: free. You cannot legally work without this.
  • 2. Bank Account:** Open an account within your first week. AIB, Bank of Ireland, and Revolut are most foreigner-friendly. You'll need: passport, PPS (temporary number fine), proof of address (utility bill, rental agreement, or letter from landlord), and your employment contract. Processing takes 3-5 working days. Fees are minimal (€0-5 monthly). Wise is excellent for transferring money to Portugal—€2.50 per transfer plus real exchange rates, versus banks charging 3-4% markup.
  • 3. Healthcare:** Ireland has mixed public (HSE) and private systems. Register with a GP first—it's free and covers basic care. Prescription costs €5 per item regardless of actual cost (capped). For serious issues or faster appointments, private insurance costs €80-150 monthly (VHI, Laya, Allianz). Many employers offer group private insurance—check your contract. Dental and optometry aren't covered publicly; budget €80-150 for dental check-ups.
  • 4. Driving Licence Transfer:** If you hold a valid Portuguese licence, you can drive in Ireland immediately, but you should exchange it for an Irish one. Visit your local driving licence centre with your Portuguese licence, proof of address, passport, and €55. Takes 20-30 minutes. Your Portuguese licence remains valid until you exchange. New Irish licence takes 2-3 weeks by post. Not urgent but do it within three months to avoid complications with car insurance.
  • 5. Portugal-Specific Considerations:** Irish employers recognise EU qualifications but you may need to register with professional bodies. Teachers, nurses, accountants, and engineers need to join their respective Irish councils (teaching: Teaching Council, nursing: NMBI, engineering: Engineers Ireland). This takes 4-8 weeks and costs €100-300. Language matters less—English at work is standard, though speaking Portuguese opens doors in tech and hospitality sectors where many Portuguese work.

Money & cost of living in Ireland

  • Monthly Budget for One Person in Dublin:**
  • Rent (one-bedroom):* €950 city centre, €750 suburbs (Ballymun, Tallaght, Swords). Outside Dublin (Cork, Galway): €600-700.
  • Groceries:* €280-350 monthly. Lidl and Aldi are cheapest; Tesco mid-range. A coffee is €2.50, lunch sandwich €4.50, dinner out €18-25. Portuguese food costs double (olive oil €6-8/litre vs Portugal €2).
  • Utilities (electricity, gas, water):* €120-150 monthly. Winter heating costs spike October-March; budget €180-200 then. Internet: €40-60 monthly.
  • Transport:* Leap Card monthly pass (all buses/trams): €110. If driving, petrol costs €1.40/litre and car insurance €800-1,200 yearly. Parking in city: €2.50/hour or €30-50 monthly in residential zones.
  • Phone:* €20-30 monthly with Three or Vodafone for unlimited data.
  • Eating out:* €150-200 monthly if socialising twice weekly. Pints cost €5-6, wine €6-8 per glass.
  • Entertainment (gym, streaming, hobbies):* €60-80 monthly.
  • Health insurance (private):* €100-150 monthly; public GP visits free after registration.
  • Budget Breakdown:**
  • **Tight budget (minimal life):** €1,800-2,000 monthly
  • **Comfortable (normal life, eating out weekly):** €2,500-2,800 monthly
  • **Good lifestyle (regular socialising, hobbies, travel):** €3,200-3,500 monthly
  • Sending Money to Portugal:**
  • Use Wise (formerly TransferWise): €2.50 flat fee + real mid-market rates. Transfers take 1-2 working days. Revolut: similar fees, faster (sometimes same-day). Banks charge 3-4% markup plus €15-25 per transfer—avoid. OFX (for larger amounts): 1.5% fee, good for €5,000+. Never use Western Union—30% markup.
  • Tax Treaty:** Portugal and Ireland have a bilateral tax treaty preventing double taxation. File taxes in Ireland if resident; if you maintain property or income in Portugal, declare it on Irish tax return. Failing to declare triggers penalties. Standard Irish income tax: 20% up to €40,000, then 40%. Portuguese residents pay 15% up to €650,000 (NHB scheme if applicable), but you lose this status upon Irish residency.

Finding work in Ireland

  • Top 5 Hiring Sectors:**
  • 1. Technology:** Highest demand. Dublin, Cork, Galway have major tech hubs (Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Facebook offices). Average salary: €55,000-75,000 entry-level, €80,000-120,000+ senior. Portuguese tech workers are highly valued—many Portuguese already work here, creating community and mentorship. No language barrier at work; English is standard.
  • 2. Healthcare:** Chronic nurse shortage. Nurses earn €35,000-45,000 base, up to €55,000+ with experience. Pharmacists: €45,000-60,000. Doctors: €70,000-90,000+ depending on specialisation. Requires registration with NMBI (Nursing and Midwifery Board) or Medical Council—adds 4-8 weeks but process is smooth for EU qualified staff.
  • 3. Finance/Accounting:** Dublin is a financial hub. Junior accountants: €35,000-42,000. Senior roles: €60,000-90,000+. Big Four firms (Deloitte, EY, KPMG, PWC) actively hire EU citizens. Requires membership with Chartered Accountants Ireland (CAI) if practising—Portuguese CPA qualifies but needs registration (€200-300, 2-4 weeks).
  • 4. Hospitality/Food Service:** Highest turnover, easiest entry. Chefs: €28,000-40,000. Restaurant managers: €35,000-45,000. Hotel work: €25,000-35,000 base plus tips. Portuguese reputation here is strong—many Portuguese restaurants and hotels in Dublin specifically hire Portuguese staff. Fastest path to employment if you speak English reasonably well.
  • 5. Skilled Trades:** Electricians, plumbers, carpenters earn €40,000-60,000+ and have constant demand. Requires Irish registration/certification; EU qualifications sometimes recognised but you may need supplementary exams (€500-2,000, 2-3 months).
  • Qualification Recognition:** EU qualifications are generally recognised under the Professional Qualifications Directive. Most professions (teaching, nursing, engineering, law) need to register with Irish professional bodies—expect €100-500 and 4-8 weeks. Language certifications (TEFL, DELTA) are universally accepted.
  • Job Search Platforms:** LinkedIn (primary), Indeed.ie, Daft.ie (has jobs section), IrishJobs.ie, and LinkedIn recruiter outreach. Portuguese speakers often get contacted directly—make that visible on your profile.
  • Interview Culture:** Very informal compared to Portugal. Expect casual dress (no suits unless banking/law), first-name basis immediately, direct questions about salary expectations early. They ask weaknesses directly; be honest but frame positively. Negotiations happen after offer. They often ask "tell me about yourself"—prepare a 2-minute story.
  • Portugal Experience Value:** Highly valued. Mention any cross-border experience, EU project work, or managing complexity across systems. European perspective is seen as positive. However, salary expectations must align with Irish market, not Portuguese—saying "I earned €30,000 in Portugal" won't anchor your negotiation upward.
  • After-Tax Reality:** Entry-level (€35,000): take-home €27,500 yearly (€2,292 monthly). Mid-level (€50,000): €37,500 yearly (€3,125 monthly). Senior (€75,000): €53,000 yearly (€4,417 monthly). These are after income tax and PRSI (social insurance, 4%). You can live on entry-level if you're frugal outside Dublin; in Dublin it's tight.

Life in Ireland — what to expect

  • Weather Reality:** Ireland is genuinely wet and grey. November-February: rain 40% of days, dark by 4pm, average 5°C. Coming from Portugal's reliable sun is a shock. Invest in a good rain jacket (€80-150), waterproof boots, and accept that "casual rain" means you go out anyway. Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD) is real—many expats supplement vitamin D (€5-10 monthly). Summer (June-August) is lovely: 15-18°C, long evenings, but still unpredictable. This is the number one lifestyle adjustment Portuguese people cite.
  • Housing Market:** Dublin is expensive and competitive. Good apartments vanish within hours. Red flags: asking for deposits without legal tenancy paperwork, pressure to decide same day, "landlord abroad" stories often hide scams. Use Daft.ie only (verify landlord via Irish Property Services Regulatory Authority website). Outside Dublin is much calmer and 30-40% cheaper. Cork and Galway are popular with Portuguese expats—better quality of life, lower stress, 2-2.5 hour train to Dublin if needed.
  • Where Portuguese Live:** Dublin's Southside (Ranelagh, Rathmines, Ballsbridge) has high Portuguese concentration. Galway's city centre similarly. Cork's Sunday's Well area. These aren't ghettos—just natural clustering for work, food, and community. Many Portuguese restaurants and bars exist; easy to find arroz de marisco and pastéis de nata.
  • Community & Facebook Groups:** "Portuguese in Ireland" (30k+ members), city-specific groups ("Portuguese in Dublin," "Portuguese in Cork"). These are genuinely helpful—job leads, housing tips, friends. Portuguese language groups exist but English is mainly spoken. WhatsApp groups proliferate for specific interests (sports, parents, professionals).
  • Cultural Surprises:** Irish directness feels rude initially but it's normal banter. Self-deprecating humour is constant—don't take it personally. Pub culture is genuinely social (not just drinking); it's the community hub. Tipping isn't obligatory (unlike US) but €1-2 is normal in restaurants. Sarcasm and irony dominate conversation; straightforward Portuguese communication can sound abrupt. Banking and bureaucracy are frustratingly slow compared to Portugal's digital efficiency—expect paper forms and phone calls. Relationships develop quickly but can feel surface-level until you're inside the circle; then loyalty is deep.
  • Finding Portuguese Food:** Continente and Tesco have small Portuguese sections (canned fish, pastéis de nata, wine). Real groceries: try specialist Portuguese shops in Dublin (Rua Portuguese, Luso Brasil on Camden Street—€5-6 for fresh nata cakes, €7 for decent olive oil). Cork and Galway have Portuguese delis. Prepare for paying 2-3x Portuguese prices. Bacalhau is expensive (€15-18/kg) but available. Freezer sections have frozen Portuguese pastries (€3-4).
  • Transport:** Outside Dublin, you'll want a car—buses are infrequent. Dublin's public transport is comprehensive (Leap Card covers all). Driving requires adjustment: left-side driving feels weird for 2-3 weeks then becomes automatic. Petrol is expensive (€1.40/litre), insurance is high (€800-1,200 yearly due to Irish accident statistics), and speed cameras are frequent. Cycling is common and safe—Dublin has dedicated lanes. Irish roads are narrow and chaotic; people drive aggressively compared to Portugal's predictability.
  • Healthcare Quality:** Public system (HSE) works but is slow. 6-month waiting lists for non-emergency surgery are normal. GPs are good quality. Private insurance (€100-150 monthly) gets you 2-week appointments and private hospital access. Prescriptions are cheap (€5 cap). Dentistry is expensive and not subsidised—budget €100 for check-up, €400+ for fillings. Mental health support is available but often waiting-list dependent; private therapists cost €60-100 per session.
  • Expat Happiness:** Most Portuguese people love Ireland within 6-12 months. Weather is the constant complaint, but wages, opportunities, and friendliness outweigh it. Those who struggle were expecting Portugal-with-better-jobs and didn't emotionally prepare for a different lifestyle. Young professionals (25-35) adapt fastest. Families struggle more with housing costs and school systems (though Irish schools outperform Portuguese academically). Returnees often stay 3-7 years then go back to Portugal for quality of life—it's a career move, not permanent relocation for most. Your happiness depends on why you moved: career ambition? You'll thrive. Escape from problems? You'll find new ones. Adventure? You'll love it.
